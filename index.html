üìê Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM)

The GMM assumes observations are generated from a mixture of Gaussian distributions, each representing a hidden regime:

\[ p(y_t) = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \pi_k \cdot \mathcal{N}(y_t \mid \mu_k, \Sigma_k) \]
  • \( y_t \): observation at time \( t \)
  • \( \pi_k \): mixture weight of regime \( k \)
  • \( \mu_k, \Sigma_k \): mean and covariance of regime \( k \)

üìê Bayesian GMM (BGMM)

A Bayesian extension of GMM that treats mixture weights and component parameters as random variables:

\[ \pi \sim \text{Dir}(\alpha), \quad \mu_k, \Sigma_k \sim \text{NIW}(\lambda) \]

This provides uncertainty quantification and automatic complexity control.

üìê Hidden Markov Model (HMM)

Captures latent state transitions over time using a Markov process with Gaussian emissions:

\[ \mathbb{P}(z_t = j \mid z_{t-1} = i) = A_{ij}, \quad y_t \mid z_t = k \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_k, \Sigma_k) \]

Joint probability:

\[ \mathbb{P}(z_{1:T}, y_{1:T}) = \prod_{t=1}^{T} \mathbb{P}(y_t \mid z_t) \cdot \mathbb{P}(z_t \mid z_{t-1}) \]
  • \( z_t \): hidden regime at time \( t \)
  • \( y_t \): observed feature (e.g., returns)
  • \( A_{ij} \): fixed regime transition probability matrix

üìÄ Hybrid Hidden Markov Model (HMM)

This model separates transition probabilities from state emissions using distinct data types:

Transition probabilities:

\[ \mathbb{P}(z_t = j \mid z_{t-1} = i, \mathbf{x}_t^{\text{implied}}) = \frac{\exp(\mathbf{w}_{ij}^\top \mathbf{x}_t + b_{ij})}{\sum_k \exp(\mathbf{w}_{ik}^\top \mathbf{x}_t + b_{ik})} \]

State emission model:

\[ y_t \mid z_t = k \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_k, \Sigma_k) \]

Joint probability:

\[ \mathbb{P}(z_{1:T}, y_{1:T}) = \prod_{t=1}^{T} \mathbb{P}(y_t \mid z_t) \cdot \mathbb{P}(z_t \mid z_{t-1}, \mathbf{x}_t^{\text{implied}}) \]
  • \( z_t \): hidden regime at time \( t \)
  • \( y_t \): observed feature
  • \( \mathbf{x}_t^{\text{implied}} \): forward-looking inputs
  • \( \mathbf{w}_{ij}, b_{ij} \): transition model weights

This model decouples transition logic from regime state emissions using two distinct data sources:

  • Implied variables (e.g. options, FedWatch, prediction markets) inform state transitions
  • Realized features (e.g. returns, inflation, spreads) shape state distributions

Mathematical structure:

Transition probabilities (logistic/softmax conditioned on implied macro features):
\[ \mathbb{P}(z_t = j \mid z_{t-1} = i, \mathbf{x}_t^{\text{implied}}) = \frac{\exp(\mathbf{w}_{ij}^\top \mathbf{x}_t + b_{ij})} {\sum_k \exp(\mathbf{w}_{ik}^\top \mathbf{x}_t + b_{ik})} \]

Regime-specific observations:
\[ y_t \mid z_t = k \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_k, \Sigma_k) \]

Full joint probability over regimes and observations:
\[ \mathbb{P}(z_{1:T}, y_{1:T}) = \prod_{t=1}^{T} \mathbb{P}(y_t \mid z_t) \cdot \mathbb{P}(z_t \mid z_{t-1}, \mathbf{x}_t^{\text{implied}}) \]

Variable Definitions:

  • \( z_t \): hidden regime label at time \( t \)
  • \( y_t \): observed market vector (returns, rates, etc.)
  • \( \mathbf{x}_t^{\text{implied}} \): forward-looking features (e.g., vol surface, Fed expectations)
  • \( \mu_k, \Sigma_k \): mean and covariance of regime \( k \)'s emissions
  • \( \mathbf{w}_{ij}, b_{ij} \): learned transition weights between states \( i \rightarrow j \)

This framework enables adaptive regime tracking, interpretable outputs, and GPU-accelerated inference.
